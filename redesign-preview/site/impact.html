Why proactive maintenance matters

The economic and operational case gets stronger when hulls stay cleaner, more consistently, over time.

These two reference graphics help explain why more frequent intervention matters. The first compares five-year fuel-cost outcomes across cleaning strategies, and the second shows how power demand climbs when fouling is allowed to accumulate between maintenance events.

Five-year fuel cost view

This comparison shows cost per average ship over five years when operators do not clean, follow standard hull-cleaning practice, or stay much closer to an always-clean condition. The red savings marker points to the kind of gap ZIMA aims to create by keeping vessels cleaner, more consistently, than reactive alternatives.

Image credit: GEF-UNDP-IMO GloFouling Partnerships Project (2022)

Power increase over time

This curve illustrates how power and fuel demand rise when fouling is left to build over time. More frequent cleanings keep resetting the curve downward, and ZIMA is aimed at turning that sawtooth pattern into something much flatter and more stable.

Image credit: GEF-UNDP-IMO GloFouling Partnerships Project (2022)
